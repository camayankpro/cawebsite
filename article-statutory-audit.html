1. What is a Statutory Audit?

A Statutory Audit is a legally mandated examination of a company's financial records, books of accounts, and financial statements by an independent Chartered Accountant (the Statutory Auditor), appointed as per the provisions of the Companies Act. The auditor's objective is to express an independent opinion on whether the financial statements present a true and fair view of the company's financial position and performance, and whether they comply with applicable accounting standards and legal requirements.

The audit report issued by the statutory auditor is a critical document â€” it accompanies the financial statements filed with the MCA (Ministry of Corporate Affairs) in the Annual Return, and is reviewed by banks, investors, regulators, and other stakeholders as an independent validation of the company's financial health.

3. Appointment of Statutory Auditor

The statutory auditor of a company is appointed by the shareholders in the Annual General Meeting (AGM). Key rules under the Companies Act:

  • First Auditor: Appointed by the Board of Directors within 30 days of incorporation. Holds office until the conclusion of the first AGM.
  • Subsequent Auditors: Appointed by shareholders at AGM for a term of 5 consecutive years â€” subject to ratification at each AGM.
  • Rotation (Large Companies): Listed companies and certain other large companies must rotate their auditors every 5â€“10 years â€” an individual CA cannot continue as auditor for more than 5 consecutive years; a CA firm cannot continue for more than 10 years.
  • Independence: The auditor must be independent â€” cannot be a director, officer, or employee of the company, or have any financial interest in the company.
  • ADT-1 Filing: The company must file Form ADT-1 with the MCA within 15 days of auditor appointment.

5. The Statutory Audit Report

The audit report contains the auditor's opinion on the financial statements. Types of audit opinions:

  • Unmodified (Clean) Opinion: The financial statements give a true and fair view â€” the best outcome for a company.
  • Qualified Opinion: The statements give a true and fair view except for a specific matter â€” the auditor has a reservation on one or more issues.
  • Adverse Opinion: The financial statements do not give a true and fair view â€” a serious red flag for stakeholders.
  • Disclaimer of Opinion: The auditor is unable to form an opinion due to significant limitations in the scope of audit.
