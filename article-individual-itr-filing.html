1. What is ITR Filing?

Income Tax Return (ITR) filing is the process by which an individual declares their total income earned during a financial year to the Income Tax Department of India and pays the applicable tax on it. It is an annual obligation and serves as an official record of your earnings, taxes paid, and tax-saving investments.

The Income Tax Department processes your return and either raises a demand (if more tax is due) or issues a refund (if excess tax has been deducted). Filing an accurate return on time ensures you stay on the right side of the law and also gives you access to several financial benefits.

2. Who Must File ITR?

While tax payment is mandatory above certain income levels, ITR filing is required in a wider set of circumstances:

  • Income Exceeds Basic Exemption: Individuals with total income exceeding the basic exemption limit (currently ‚Çπ2.5 lakh under old regime; ‚Çπ3 lakh under new regime for individuals below 60 years) must file.
  • Senior Citizens (60‚Äì79 years): Basic exemption of ‚Çπ3 lakh (old regime); super senior citizens (80+) get ‚Çπ5 lakh.
  • Foreign Asset Holders: Any resident individual holding foreign assets or having signing authority in foreign accounts must file, regardless of income level.
  • Refund Claimants: If TDS has been deducted on your income and you want a refund, filing is mandatory.
  • High-Value Transactions: Depositing more than ‚Çπ1 crore in a current account, spending ‚Çπ2 lakh+ on foreign travel, or electricity bill exceeding ‚Çπ1 lakh triggers mandatory filing.
  • Business or Profession Income: All individuals earning from business or profession must file.
  • Capital Gains: Any individual with capital gains from shares, mutual funds, property, etc., must file.
  • Carry Forward of Losses: To carry forward losses (capital, business, etc.) to future years, filing is compulsory.

4. Documents Required for Individual ITR Filing

The documents vary based on your income sources, but the commonly needed ones include:

For Salaried Individuals:

  • Form 16 (issued by your employer) ‚Äî Part A and Part B
  • Salary slips for the financial year
  • Form 26AS and Annual Information Statement (AIS)
  • PAN Card
  • Bank account details (all accounts)

For Income from House Property:

  • Rental receipts or rent agreement
  • Municipal tax receipts
  • Home loan interest certificate from bank

For Capital Gains:

  • Stock broker capital gain statement
  • Mutual fund consolidated account statement
  • Sale deed and purchase agreement for property

For Tax-Saving Investments (Deductions):

  • LIC premium receipts, ELSS statements (Section 80C)
  • PPF passbook, NSC certificates
  • Health insurance premium receipts (Section 80D)
  • Home loan interest certificate (Section 24b)
  • Donation receipts (Section 80G)
  • NPS contribution proof (Section 80CCD)

6. Filing Deadlines & Penalties for Late Filing

ITR filing deadlines are fixed each year by the Income Tax Department. While the exact dates may vary year to year due to government extensions, the standard deadlines for a financial year ending March 31 are:

CategoryStandard Due Date
Individuals not requiring audit31st July of the assessment year
Individuals requiring tax audit31st October of the assessment year
Individuals with transfer pricing matters30th November of the assessment year
Belated / Revised Return31st December of the assessment year

Penalties for Late Filing:

  • Late Filing Fee (Section 234F): ‚Çπ1,000 if income is up to ‚Çπ5 lakh; ‚Çπ5,000 for income above ‚Çπ5 lakh.
  • Interest on Tax Due (Section 234A): 1% per month on outstanding tax from the due date.
  • Loss of Carry Forward: You lose the right to carry forward business, capital, or speculation losses if you file late.
  • Prosecution: For wilful non-filing or tax evasion, imprisonment of 3 months to 7 years is possible.

7. Benefits of Filing ITR on Time

  • Timely Refunds: Faster processing and quicker refunds credited directly to your bank account.
  • Loan Applications: Banks and NBFCs require ITR for 2‚Äì3 years for home loans, personal loans, and business loans.
  • Visa Processing: Most embassies require ITR copies as proof of financial standing.
  • Government Tenders: Many tenders require ITR proof for eligibility.
  • Insurance Coverage: Higher insurance coverage often requires ITR as income proof.
  • Carry Forward of Losses: Losses (short-term capital, long-term capital, business) can be set off against future gains.
  • Compliance Shield: Protects you from scrutiny notices and penalty proceedings.

8. Common Mistakes to Avoid in ITR Filing

  • Wrong ITR Form: Always match your income sources to the correct form ‚Äî filing the wrong form makes your return defective.
  • Not Reporting All Income: Interest from savings, FDs, and post office accounts, dividends, and rental income must all be disclosed.
  • Mismatch with Form 26AS / AIS: Any mismatch between your return and Form 26AS can trigger a notice.
  • Forgetting Employer PAN: For salaried individuals, entering the correct employer TAN/PAN is essential.
  • Missing Foreign Income / Assets: Not disclosing foreign income or assets attracts penalties under Black Money Act.
  • Not Verifying the Return: An ITR is not complete until it is e-verified. Unverified returns are treated as not filed.
  • Choosing Wrong Tax Regime: Not evaluating old vs. new regime before filing can cost you significant tax savings.
